Quick Case Studies

Role

UX Director

Why this project is important to me

When EVERFI partnered with the Truth Initiative, I led a team of designers, researchers, writers, and developers tasked with making a dynamic course that would speak to high school students about the dangers of vaping. Working with teens, we developed an experience and message that addressed the realities of addiction while revealing the marketing practices of vaping companies.

Working with teens to tackle vaping

Discovery

We met virtually with teens from SWAT (Students Working Against Tobacco) to discuss tone and experience and surveyed high school teachers and students.

Findings

Data from surveys, interviews, and user tests revealed there was too much copy to scroll and not enough interactivity. Teens told us to focus on data and facts, not trying to sound cool.

Next steps

The content team reworked copy and UX worked closely with developers to prototype experiences that felt more game-like and less like a workbook. Unique perspectives culled from research generated new ideas.

Validate

Further testing confirmed shorter text blocks, data-driven stories, and a new card game we were developing increased engagement. Testing focused our solutions, giving the team confidence in our solutions.

Build

The findings from research and discovery reduced development time and bugs. There were no major pivots or rebuilds and testing helped gain insights that impacted our entire product suite.

The problem

We measure digital learning efficacy with assessments, but assessments can feel tideous and stressful for a lot of users. Our research revealed that many learners skimmed question and answer text, and users guessing answers was not uncommon. There seemed to be little incentive for learners to engage. Our team was tasked to investigate and improve the experience.

Desktop-first design

Long text blocks that many users would skim, no progress indicator to show number of questions, and long-scrolls with multiple clicks.

Screen 1

The first assessment screen would typically have a lot of copy, no instructions, and no indication of progress.

Screen 3

If the answer was incorrect, users would receive feedback, then select the retry button. Testing revealed a lot of guessing at this stage.

Discover

User testing revealed the question and answer text to be too long in most cases, prompting learners to skim. The assessment section templates were originally designed for desktops. Threy would span multiple screens, often times requiring scrolling, and had no indication of progress. Our courses told a story and had characters, but the assessments didn't include those elements and took the learner out of the story.

Develop

Since we had a fully-developed design system that was mirrored in the code,we were able to prototype and test our ideas rapidly. After multiple-rounds of testing with students that inspired creative development, shorter text, and clear UI, we had a found a solution that was accessible, responsive, and fun.

Mobile-first design

Short, punchy copy, progress indicated by fun icon groups, and fewer clicks. The development team made certain that everything was labeled, focusable, and that states were properly announced.

Screen 1

Introduction screen with instructions to help users transition from passive learning to active engagement.

Screen 2

Less form, more game. Hearts used to show progress and correct / incorrect responses.

Screen 3

No more scrolling! Feedback dynamically takes over the screen and prompts user to continue.

Screen 4

Users were more likely to self-select to retry if they didn't get a perfect score, increasing engagement.

Validate

Future course builds looked to incorporate our assessment solution. The course that premiered the card game interaction received the 2021 Silver Halo Award.

Role

UX Director

Why this project is important to me

Inclusive design is always the goal. While at EVERFI, I worked with the VP of Accessibility, our Senior UX Researcher, and developers from Fable to test our design sytem components with people with disabilities. This research improved our component library and patterns while teaching us how to plan and build sophisticated interactions without sacrificing Accessibility.

Discover

After deciding to work with Fable, our first task was to decide were to begin investigating. Instead of testing individual products, we started with our design system components.

Develop

I gathered my personas, flows, and findings and started wireframing. After a design review, I prototyped proposed solutions in the code using our design system and platform components. I was able to test my ideas with customers at local Fool events and receive feedback that sharpened my work.

I teamed with engineers to find a multi-step solution that worked with our payment system (an engineer found a way to validate payment). Then I collaborated with Leadership, Sales, and Marketing to bring more clarity to membership renewal.

The UI solutions were fairly straight-forward (I had a ton of help from users). Navigating system limitations and departmental needs was more challenging. Ultimately, we found a guided, multi-step solution that rendered payment, and added clarity with UI and instructional text updates.

Validate

Payment completion increased by 30% after the update was deployed.
