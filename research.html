Finding functional and practicable solutions

UX research helps discover what users find valuable, as well as what frustrates them. Data and user insights are helpful at every phase of the product cycle, but have the most impact when research is applied at the start.

The early findings from user surveys, interviews, and lo-fi prototype tests can uncover potential issues and bugs that haven't been built yet, saving development and design time. This is great! This process allows teams to discover and scale successful ideas as the product develops.

Also, people seem more willing to share their observations if the work appears unfinished. (Perhaps critiquing something that looks polished and complete seems rude to some?) Showing someone your messy work has its own set of rewards.

Discover

Whether you're updating an existing idea or searching for a new solution, a great first step is to start talking with potential users. Qualitative research is great at this stage. Asking users open-ended questions or observing user tests helps when exploring new ideas. Again, it doesn't have to be anything fancy. All you need is something that gives the user an opportunity to provide feedback — a paper prototype, a script draft, or a Google Form survey with static images works.

The value in Discovery is not only generating new ideas, but also validating them. UX research is about finding what's working, as well as what's not. Knowing what to leave out as you move into the development phase is as valuable as knowing the good ideas. (Why build a complex interaction that requires multiple hours of development time if nobody uses it?)

Testing at the beginning saves time

Imagine a scenario where user testing reveals the back button on the screen to the left is difficult to locate.

Testing reveals moving it out of the header and beside the next button is preferable.

Solving this issue requires one designer and little time. And this solution scales, as it impacts virtually every screen in the product.

Discovering this early saves the team countless design and development hours.

Testing only at the end is risky

If this issue is discovered after the product has been launched, it will require design and development time to solve.

If the design system doesn't include components, then every screen will need to be updated. Yikes!

Instead of a single designer updating a wireframe, fixing the issue at this stage will require more people and effort.

To add insult to injury, the time spent building the original idea was essentially wasted.

Develop

Once you're working in the code, you'd prefer the pivots to be minimal and few. At this stage, you're building screens with design system elements, updating copy, and bringing the experience to life in a staging enviroment. Using quantitive research can help tighten everything. For example, measuring time on task and error/success rates can help you choose between solution options or quickly validate new ideas.

As a product comes together, UX research will save development time (in my experience, a lot of it) and leave few questions to be answered as you ready to deploy.

Validate

When a product is deployed, data analytics and further user testing can help validate the success of the experience. Checking in with Sales and Marketing can shine a light on friction and success. After a successful launch, you're looking for improvements that not only help the product, but also scale to other ones.
